I've been circling around music technology for years without actually starting. Today I'm starting.

The short version of the plan: learn DSP from the ground up, learn C++, build audio plugins, and document everything as I go. Broader than that &mdash; actually engage with the music industry itself. Policy. Economics. The machinery underneath streaming. Not just the code.

The specific aim I've landed on is the intersection most people in music tech don't actually occupy: engineers who don't really listen to music, and music people who can't read code. I have enough of both to sit in between if I put the work in. Discovery algorithms, recommendation systems, the MIR research that sits underneath something like Spotify's Discover Weekly. UPF's Music Technology Group in Barcelona is the target for a Masters in roughly fifteen months, and the thesis is the entry point.

## Why this blog exists

Two reasons, neither of them about an audience.

**Explaining forces understanding.** I can't write a post about convolution without actually understanding convolution. The blog is a study tool dressed up as a public artefact.

**Trail.** By the time I apply to UPF I want to have a year-plus of visible work. Not a portfolio crammed together the week before applications open. The documentation is part of the work, not something I do after.

So this starts before the work is good. The first sine wave counts.
